Growing in Grace:

Sanctification, Obedience, and Spiritual Fruit
A 70-Minute Teaching Session

Introduction: Why This Matters

(5 mins)

Good evening, everyone! I'm so glad you're here tonight. You know, I've been thinking a lot lately about how we grow as Christians. Have you ever wondered why some people seem to have this deep joy and peace, while others who've been in church for years still struggle with the same issues? Or why some Christians naturally overflow with kindness and patience, while others seem stuck?

Tonight, we're going to talk about one of the most important—yet often misunderstood—aspects of the Christian life: sanctification. That's a fancy word that simply means "how God changes us after we're saved."

I chose this topic because I believe many of us are confused about how spiritual growth actually works. Some of us are exhausted from trying to earn God's love through good behavior. Others wonder if they're really saved because they still struggle with sin. And some are just stuck, not seeing the growth they long for.

The beautiful truth is that God has a plan for transforming us, and it's not about us trying harder—it's about understanding how His grace works in our daily lives. So let's dive in together.

Time Breakdown (Total: 70 mins)

  • Welcome & Introduction: 5 min
  • Segment 1: Sanctification: 20 min
  • Segment 2: The Heart of Obedience: 15 min
  • Segment 3: Spiritual Fruit: 15 min
  • Discussions: 13 min
  • Closing & Prayer: 2 min

Segment 1: Understanding Sanctification (20 mins)

What Sanctification Really Is

Let me start with a story. Imagine you adopt a child who's been living on the streets. The moment you sign those papers, that child is legally yours—fully part of your family. But they still have street habits, right? They might hide food in their room, flinch when you move too quickly, or struggle to trust your love. Over time, as they experience your consistent care, they begin to change. They stop hoarding food, they relax in your presence, they learn what it means to be loved.

That's a picture of sanctification. When God saves us, we're instantly His children—that's justification, and it's complete. But we still have old patterns, old wounds, old ways of thinking. Sanctification is God's patient, loving work of transforming us to actually live like the children we already are.

John Piper puts it beautifully: "The only sin we can fight against successfully is a forgiven sin... Justification is the foundation of sanctification." In other words, we don't change in order to become God's children—we change because we already are.

Let me share some Scripture that unpacks this:

2 Corinthians 3:18 - "And we all, who with unveiled faces contemplate the Lord's glory, are being transformed into his image with ever-increasing glory, which comes from the Lord, who is the Spirit."

Notice the passive voice—"are being transformed." We're not transforming ourselves. God is doing the transforming. But also notice we're actively involved—we're contemplating His glory.

How Sanctification Works

Think of sanctification like learning to play an instrument. When you first pick up a guitar, your fingers don't naturally know where to go. It feels awkward, even painful. But with practice, guided by a teacher, something amazing happens. Eventually, your fingers move without you even thinking about it. Music just flows.

R.C. Sproul explains: "Sanctification is the work of God's free grace, whereby we are renewed in the whole man after the image of God, and are enabled more and more to die unto sin and live unto righteousness."

Three key truths about how this works:

1. It's God's Work, Not Ours

Philippians 2:13 - "For it is God who works in you to will and to act in order to fulfill his good purpose."

We're not passive, but God is the primary actor. He's the sculptor; we're the marble.

2. It Happens Through Union with Christ

Romans 6:5 - "For if we have been united with him in a death like his, we will certainly also be united with him in a resurrection like his."

Because we're connected to Jesus, His life flows into ours. Like a branch connected to a vine, we receive His life-giving power.

3. It's a Process, Not an Event

Philippians 1:6 - "Being confident of this, that he who began a good work in you will carry it on to completion until the day of Christ Jesus."

Unlike justification (which happens instantly), sanctification takes our whole lifetime.

Why Understanding This Matters

Here's why this is so crucial: Many Christians live in one of two ditches. Some think they have to earn God's love through good behavior—that's exhausting and impossible. Others think that since they're saved by grace, it doesn't matter how they live—that's cheap grace, and it's not biblical.

Jonathan Edwards captured the balance: "True grace is of a transforming nature... The grace of God in the heart is a holy seed that will certainly grow up to eternal life."

Charles Spurgeon adds this powerful insight: "Christ will be master of the heart, and sin must be mortified. If your life is unholy, then your heart is unchanged, and you are an unsaved person. The Savior will sanctify His people, renew them, give them a hatred of sin, and a love of holiness."

Discussion Questions (choose one):

  • "When you were growing up, did you ever feel like you had to be 'good enough' for your parents? How is God different?"
  • "What's one thing you do just because you love someone, not because you have to?"

Segment 2: The Heart of Obedience (15 mins)

Love as the Root of Obedience

Now, let's talk about obedience and good works. This is where that adoption story really helps us understand.

Remember that adopted child? Once they truly understand they're safe and loved, something beautiful happens. They start following the family rules not because they have to earn their place, but because they trust their parents and want to please them. They help with chores not to become part of the family, but because they already are.

Voddie Baucham says it perfectly: "Our love will be shown by obedience, not by how good we feel about God at a given moment. The Gospel produces obedience. If you love Him, you will keep His commandments."

Look at what Jesus Himself said:

John 14:15 - "If you love me, keep my commands."

Notice He didn't say, "Keep my commands so that I'll love you." He said, "If you love me"—the love comes first, then obedience naturally follows.

Understanding Good Works

This brings us to one of the most misunderstood passages in the Bible:

Ephesians 2:8-10 - "For it is by grace you have been saved, through faith—and this is not from yourselves, it is the gift of God—not by works, so that no one can boast. For we are God's handiwork, created in Christ Jesus to do good works, which God prepared in advance for us to do."

See the order? Saved by grace (verse 8-9), created for good works (verse 10). Works don't produce salvation; salvation produces works.

John Owen, the great Puritan, warned: "Be killing sin, or sin will be killing you." But notice—he's talking to people who are already Christians. We fight sin and pursue obedience not to become God's children, but because we are.

The Freedom of Gospel Obedience

Here's what's so freeing about this: When you understand that your obedience flows from love rather than fear, everything changes. You're not constantly wondering, "Have I done enough?" Instead, you're asking, "How can I express my gratitude to the One who saved me?"

Think about it this way: If someone rescued you from a burning building, you wouldn't spend the rest of your life trying to pay them back. But you might send them a card every year on the anniversary, name your child after them, or tell everyone about their heroism. That's gratitude, not payment.

C.S. Lewis understood this when he wrote: "Good actions are not the root; they are the fruit of a changed heart."

Discussion Question:

"How does understanding obedience as gratitude rather than payment change your daily walk with God?"

Segment 3: The Natural Growth of Spiritual Fruit (15 mins)

Fruit, Not Manufacturing

Now, let's talk about the fruit of the Spirit. This is where many of us get tripped up. We read Galatians 5:22-23 like it's a to-do list:

Galatians 5:22-23 - "But the fruit of the Spirit is love, joy, peace, forbearance, kindness, goodness, faithfulness, gentleness and self-control. Against such things there is no law."

And then we try to manufacture these qualities: "Okay, today I'm going to be more joyful. Tomorrow I'll work on patience." But that's like tying apples onto a dead tree and calling it alive.

The Vine and the Branches

Jesus gives us a better picture:

John 15:4-5 - "Remain in me, as I also remain in you. No branch can bear fruit by itself; it must remain in the vine. Neither can you bear fruit unless you remain in me. I am the vine; you are the branches. If you remain in me and I in you, you will bear much fruit; apart from me you can do nothing."

Notice what Jesus doesn't say. He doesn't say, "Try really hard to produce fruit." He says, "Remain in me." The fruit is the natural result of staying connected to Him.

Think about a healthy apple tree. It doesn't strain to produce apples. It doesn't have apple-producing meetings or set apple goals. It simply draws nutrients from the soil, receives sunshine and rain, and naturally produces fruit. That's its nature.

Diagnosing Spiritual Health

Here's where this gets practical. If you're not seeing growth in love, joy, peace, and the other fruit, the solution isn't to try harder. The solution is to examine your connection to the vine.

R.C. Sproul explained it this way: "A bad tree cannot bear good fruit. This is why we need to be made new creatures, with new hearts... Out of the heart that has been made new comes the fruit of obedience."

Ask yourself:

  • Am I spending time in God's Word, letting it nourish my soul?
  • Am I talking to God in prayer, maintaining that vital connection?
  • Am I in fellowship with other believers who encourage my growth?
  • Am I confessing sin quickly, removing barriers between me and God?

A lack of spiritual fruit isn't a call to try harder—it's a call to draw closer.

Faith and Works Together

This brings us to James's challenging words:

James 2:17 - "In the same way, faith by itself, if it is not accompanied by action, is dead."

James 2:26 - "As the body without the spirit is dead, so faith without deeds is dead."

James isn't contradicting Paul. He's saying that real faith—living faith—naturally produces works, just like a living tree naturally produces fruit. If there's no fruit over time, we need to ask whether the tree is truly alive.

Jonathan Edwards put it powerfully: "The degree of love to God may be known by the degree of our hatred of sin."

Discussion Questions (choose one):

  • "Think of a plant in your house or garden. What does it need to grow well? How is that like our spiritual life?"
  • "Share about a time when you tried really hard to change something about yourself. What happened?"

Discussion (13 mins)

The Reality Check (5 mins)

Let's address something important. Many people today say they have a "relationship with God" but their lives show little evidence of transformation. They claim to know Him but rarely read His Word, seldom gather with His people, and their habits remain unchanged.

John 10:27 says: "My sheep listen to my voice; I know them, and they follow me."

Think about this: How can we expect to recognize His voice if we never spend time listening? How can we follow if we're not walking with Him?

Hebrews 10:25 warns us: "Not giving up meeting together, as some are in the habit of doing, but encouraging one another—and all the more as you see the Day approaching."

Our Partnership with God (5 mins)

Remember, sanctification is God's work, but we're not passive. God has given us means of grace—tools He uses to transform us:

1 Thessalonians 5:23-24 - "May God himself, the God of peace, sanctify you through and through... The one who calls you is faithful, and he will do it."

Notice: God does it, but through means!

Romans 12:1-2 - "Therefore, I urge you, brothers and sisters, in view of God's mercy, to offer your bodies as a living sacrifice, holy and pleasing to God—this is your true and proper worship. Do not conform to the pattern of this world, but be transformed by the renewing of your mind."

We offer ourselves; God does the transforming. We read His Word; He renews our minds. We gather with believers; He encourages our hearts.

Charles Spurgeon put it perfectly: "Sanctification grows out of faith in Jesus Christ. Remember holiness is a flower, not a root; it is not sanctification that saves, but salvation that sanctifies."

Application Questions (3 mins)

  1. Quick Check: "In your own words, what's one thing that stood out to you tonight?"
  2. Personal Story: "Can anyone share a simple example of how God has changed you since you became a Christian? Maybe a bad habit that's gotten better or a new desire to help others?"
  3. Honest Reflection: "Are you truly connected to the vine, or just going through the motions? What evidence of growth do you see in your life?"

Closing Prayer (2 mins)

Let's close by thanking God for His patient, transforming work in our lives:

"Father, we're amazed by Your grace. Thank You that You don't just forgive us and leave us unchanged. Thank You for Your patient work of making us more like Jesus. Help us to rest in Your love, to respond with grateful obedience, and to remain connected to Christ our vine. Where we've been trying to transform ourselves, give us the humility to let You do Your work. Where we've been passive, stir us to actively abide in You. We trust You to complete what You've begun. In Jesus' name, Amen."

Charles Spurgeon reminds us: "If he gives you the grace to make you believe, he will give you the grace to live a holy life afterward." Trust Him for both!
