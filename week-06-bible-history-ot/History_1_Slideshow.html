The Incredible Unity of Scripture üìö

Think about this for a moment: The Bible contains 66 different books, written by 40 different authors, over a span of 1,500 years. These writers came from every walk of life - kings, fishermen, tax collectors, shepherds, doctors.

Letter after letter arrives, each one revealing more about his character, his love for her, his plans for their future together.

By the time their wedding day arrives, she feels like she already knows him. She's fallen in love through his words. That's exactly what God is doing through Scripture - preparing us to meet our Bridegroom face to face!

A Word That Changes Everything: HESED ‚ù§Ô∏è

Before we dive into the stories, I need to teach you one Hebrew word that will transform how you read the Bible: hesed (pronounced "HEH-sed").

This word appears over 240 times in the Old Testament, and English Bibles usually translate it as "steadfast love" or "lovingkindness." But those translations don't capture its full meaning.

9/95

What Hesed Really Means

Hesed is covenant love - the kind of love that makes promises and keeps them no matter what. It's not based on feelings or circumstances. It's loyal, faithful, unbreakable love.

When God says He has hesed love for you, He's saying: "I have bound Myself to you with an unbreakable promise, and nothing you do can make Me let go."

10/95

What Is a Covenant? ü§ù

A covenant is more than a contract. A contract is about exchanging goods or services - if you break it, you pay a penalty and move on. But a covenant is about relationship. It's a binding promise between persons who pledge loyalty to each other.

"A covenant is a sacred bond, sovereignly administered, with life-and-death consequences" - R.C. Sproul
11/95

Two Types of Covenants

Conditional Covenants: "If you do this, then I'll do that." These depend on both parties keeping their promises.

Unconditional Covenants: "I will do this, period." These depend only on the promise-maker, not the recipient.

Watch for this pattern tonight - it becomes very important!

üçé The First Covenant & The Fall

Where Everything Went Wrong

13/95

In the Beginning... üåÖ

Picture this: God creates a perfect world. No death, no pain, no struggle. He forms Adam from the dust and breathes life into him. God plants a gorgeous garden in Eden and puts Adam there to work it and care for it.

Then God says, "It's not good for man to be alone," so He creates Eve from Adam's rib - bone of his bone, flesh of his flesh. They're naked and feel no shame. Perfect intimacy with each other and with God.

14/95

God's Assessment: Very Good! ‚ú®

"God saw all that he had made, and it was very good."
- Genesis 1:31

Not just "good" - VERY good! Perfect harmony between God and humanity, husband and wife, humans and creation. This is what we were made for - unbroken fellowship with God and each other.

15/95

Just One Rule

"You are free to eat from any tree in the garden; but you must not eat from the tree of the knowledge of good and evil, for when you eat from it you will certainly die."
- Genesis 2:16-17

Notice: God gave them everything - an entire garden of delicious fruit! Just one tree was off limits. This wasn't God being mean. He was asking them to trust Him, to accept that He knows what's best.

16/95

Enter the Serpent üêç

The serpent was crafty. He comes to Eve and asks, "Did God really say you can't eat from any tree?" Notice how he twists God's words. Eve corrects him, but then the serpent tells a bold lie: "You won't die! God knows that when you eat it, you'll become like Him, knowing good and evil."

The temptation: You can be your own god. You don't need to trust Him; you can decide for yourself what's right and wrong.

17/95

The Heartbreak üíî

"When the woman saw that the fruit of the tree was good for food and pleasing to the eyes, and also desirable for gaining wisdom, she took some and ate it. She also gave some to her husband, who was with her, and he ate it."
- Genesis 3:6

In that moment, everything changed. The first covenant was shattered by human disobedience. Death entered the world. Their eyes were opened, but not in a good way - they realized they were naked and felt shame for the first time.

18/95

Paradise Lost

When God came walking in the garden, Adam and Eve hid. Think about that - they used to enjoy perfect fellowship with God, and now they're hiding behind trees. God calls out, "Where are you?" Not because He doesn't know, but to give them a chance to come clean.

Instead, Adam blames Eve AND God: "The woman YOU put here with me gave me the fruit." Eve blames the serpent. No one takes responsibility.

19/95

The Curses

To the serpent: Cursed to crawl on his belly. Enmity between him and humanity.

To the woman: Pain in childbearing. Tension in marriage relationships.

To the man: The ground is cursed. Work becomes toilsome. Eventually, death.

But hidden in the curse is the first promise of hope...

20/95

The First Gospel Promise! ‚≠ê

"I will put enmity between you and the woman, and between your offspring and hers; he will crush your head, and you will strike his heel."
- Genesis 3:15

Even in judgment, God promises hope! One day, a descendant of the woman would crush the serpent's head. Yes, the serpent would wound him ("strike his heel"), but the serpent would be defeated. This is the first promise of Jesus!

21/95

The First Sacrifice ü©∏

"The Lord God made garments of skin for Adam and his wife and clothed them."
- Genesis 3:21

Don't miss this! To make garments of skin, animals had to die. This is the first death in God's perfect world - innocent animals dying to cover human shame. God Himself performs the first sacrifice, teaching them that sin brings death, but He will provide a covering.

Every animal sacrifice from this point forward points to Jesus, the ultimate sacrifice.

22/95

Cast Out of the Garden

God expels them from Eden and places cherubim with flaming swords to guard the way to the Tree of Life. This seems harsh, but it's actually merciful - if they ate from the Tree of Life in their sinful state, they'd live forever separated from God.

The rest of the Bible is the story of how God makes a way for humanity to return to His presence.

23/95

From Bad to Worse üìâ

Things spiral downward quickly. Adam and Eve's son Cain becomes jealous of his brother Abel. God accepts Abel's sacrifice but rejects Cain's. Why? Abel brought the best of his flock in faith; Cain just brought "some" of his crops - no faith, no heart in it.

God warns Cain: "Sin is crouching at your door; it desires to have you, but you must rule over it." But Cain doesn't listen. He lures Abel into a field and murders him. The first human death is a brother killing a brother.

24/95

Violence Fills the Earth

"The Lord saw how great the wickedness of the human race had become on the earth, and that every inclination of the thoughts of the human heart was only evil all the time."
- Genesis 6:5

By Noah's time, things had gotten so bad that people's thoughts were ONLY evil ALL the time. Violence, corruption, and wickedness filled the earth. Humanity had completely turned away from their Creator.

25/95

God's Heart Breaks

"The Lord regretted that he had made human beings on the earth, and his heart was deeply troubled."
- Genesis 6:6

This doesn't mean God made a mistake. It means He grieved over what humanity had become. Like a parent watching their child destroy their life with drugs, God's heart was broken over the creatures He loved.

26/95

üåà Noah and Starting Over

God's Judgment and Mercy

28/95

One Righteous Man üôè

In a world gone completely evil, one man stood out: Noah. The Bible says he "found favor in the eyes of the Lord" and "walked faithfully with God." God comes to Noah with an unbelievable assignment: build a massive boat because He's going to flood the entire earth.

Think about this - Noah likely had never seen rain (Genesis 2:6 suggests the earth was watered by mist). Yet God says build a boat 450 feet long, 75 feet wide, and 45 feet high. That's one and a half football fields long!

29/95

120 Years of Preaching

It probably took Noah decades to build the ark. Every day, as he worked, people would have mocked him. "Crazy Noah, building a boat where there's no water!" But Noah kept building and kept preaching, warning people that judgment was coming.

God was patient - He gave humanity 120 years to repent while Noah built. But no one listened except Noah's family.

30/95

This Wasn't a Children's Story üö´

We often see cute pictures of Noah's ark with smiling animals. But this was the most devastating judgment in human history. Every person outside the ark died - men, women, children. The world was literally washed clean of human wickedness.

This shows us how seriously God takes sin. He is patient and merciful, but He is also holy and just. Sin cannot remain in His presence.

31/95

Mercy in Judgment üíô

Yet even in this devastating judgment, God showed mercy. He saved Noah's family - eight people total. He preserved two of every kind of animal. He didn't completely destroy His creation.

Why? Because God still had a plan. Remember that promise in the garden about the woman's offspring crushing the serpent? That promise was still in effect. The Savior would come through Noah's line.

32/95

A New Beginning

After more than a year on the ark, the waters recede. Noah sends out a raven, then a dove. Finally, the dove returns with an olive leaf - new life is sprouting! When they finally leave the ark, the first thing Noah does is build an altar and offer sacrifices to God.

The Bible says God smelled the pleasing aroma and made a promise...

33/95

The Rainbow Promise üåà

"I have set my rainbow in the clouds, and it will be the sign of the covenant between me and the earth... Never again will the waters become a flood to destroy all life."
- Genesis 9:13,15

This is the first UNCONDITIONAL covenant! God doesn't say "If you're good, I won't flood the earth." He simply promises, period. The rainbow is God's signature on an unbreakable promise.

34/95

Understanding God's Covenant with Noah

Notice the details of this covenant:

  • Made with Noah AND all living creatures
  • No conditions - depends only on God
  • The sign (rainbow) is in the heavens, not on earth
  • God says He will "remember" when He sees it

This shows God's heart - even in judgment, He desires to preserve life and show mercy!

üóº Tower of Babel

Humanity's Pride Problem

‚≠ê Abraham: Father of Faith

God's Promise to Bless All Nations

40/95

An Unexpected Call

Abram (later renamed Abraham) is living in Ur, a prosperous city in modern-day Iraq. He's 75 years old, settled, comfortable. Then God speaks to him: "Leave your country, your people and your father's household and go to the land I will show you."

No GPS coordinates. No travel brochure. Just "go to the land I will show you." And here's the amazing part - Abraham went! He packed up his wife Sarah, his nephew Lot, and all their possessions and started walking.

41/95

A World-Changing Promise

"I will make you into a great nation, and I will bless you; I will make your name great, and you will be a blessing. I will bless those who bless you, and whoever curses you I will curse; and all peoples on earth will be blessed through you."
- Genesis 12:2-3

Look at that last line: "ALL peoples on earth will be blessed through you." That includes you and me! This wasn't just about Abraham - it was about God's plan to rescue the whole world.

42/95

Just One Problem...

God promises to make Abraham into a great nation. But Abraham is 75 years old and his wife Sarah is 65 - and they have no children. Sarah is barren. How can you become a great nation without any kids?

This is where faith comes in. Abraham had to trust that God could do the impossible. And he did trust... mostly. But the waiting was hard.

43/95

25 Years of Waiting

Years pass. No baby. Abraham is now in his 80s, Sarah in her 70s. They try to help God out - Sarah gives her servant Hagar to Abraham to have a child. This was culturally acceptable but not God's plan. Ishmael is born, but God says, "No, Sarah will have a son."

More years pass. Abraham is now 99, Sarah is 89. Way past childbearing age. Then three visitors (angels) appear and say, "By this time next year, Sarah will have a son." Sarah laughs - she's nearly 90!

44/95

The Miracle Baby üë∂

But God keeps His promise! At age 90, Sarah gives birth to Isaac. His name means "laughter" because Sarah said, "God has brought me laughter, and everyone who hears about this will laugh with me."

Twenty-five years of waiting, but God's timing was perfect. Abraham learned that God's delays are not God's denials.

45/95

In Genesis 15, God formally makes a covenant with Abraham. In ancient times, both parties would walk between cut animal pieces, saying, "May this happen to me if I break this covenant."

But God puts Abraham into a deep sleep. Only God (appearing as a smoking firepot and blazing torch) passes between the pieces. God is saying: "This covenant depends on Me alone. You can't break it because you're not even awake!"

46/95

The Sign of the Covenant ‚úÇÔ∏è

"You are to undergo circumcision, and it will be the sign of the covenant between me and you."
- Genesis 17:11

God gives Abraham a sign - circumcision. Every male descendant would bear this mark. Unlike the rainbow (in the sky), this sign is on their bodies - a permanent reminder that they belong to God.

Later, Paul explains that what really matters is "circumcision of the heart" - an inward change that only God can perform!

Abraham's heart must have been breaking as he answers: "God himself will provide the lamb for the burnt offering, my son." More prophetic than Abraham knew!

49/95

Abraham builds the altar, arranges the wood, binds Isaac, and raises the knife. At the last second, an angel stops him: "Now I know that you fear God, because you have not withheld from me your son, your only son."

Because the true Lamb was still to come. Every ram and lamb sacrificed from that day forward would point to Jesus, the Lamb of God who takes away the sin of the world!

üë®‚Äçüë¶‚Äçüë¶ The Covenant Family Grows

Isaac, Jacob, and Joseph

53/95

God Chooses the Younger

Isaac marries Rebekah and they have twin boys: Esau and Jacob. Esau is born first, making him the heir. He's a rough, outdoorsy man - a hunter. Jacob is quiet, staying near the tents. But God had told Rebekah before they were born: "The older will serve the younger."

Jacob tricks Esau into selling his birthright for a bowl of stew, then later deceives his blind father Isaac to steal Esau's blessing. Jacob's name means "deceiver" - and he lives up to it!

54/95

Wrestling with God

Jacob has to flee from Esau's anger. Years later, when returning home, he's terrified Esau will kill him. The night before meeting Esau, a mysterious man wrestles with Jacob all night. As dawn breaks, the man says, "Let me go," but Jacob replies, "I will not let you go unless you bless me."

The man blesses him and changes his name from Jacob (deceiver) to Israel (one who strives with God). Jacob realizes he's been wrestling with God Himself! The deceiver becomes the father of God's chosen nation.

55/95

The Covenant Continues

"I am the Lord, the God of your father Abraham and the God of Isaac. I will give you and your descendants the land on which you are lying."
- Genesis 28:13

God appears to Jacob in a dream at Bethel and confirms that the covenant made with Abraham continues through him. The promise isn't lost despite Jacob's deception - God works through flawed people!

56/95

Twelve Sons, Twelve Tribes

Israel (Jacob) has twelve sons through four different women (two wives and two servants - it's complicated!). These twelve sons become the twelve tribes of Israel. But there's major family dysfunction...

Israel plays favorites. He loves Rachel more than Leah, and he loves Rachel's son Joseph more than all his other sons. This favoritism will cause huge problems.

57/95

The Favorite Son üß•

Joseph is seventeen when our story really begins. His father gives him a richly ornamented robe - the famous "coat of many colors." This wasn't just a nice gift; it was a sign that Joseph was being treated as the firstborn heir, even though he was son number eleven!

To make matters worse, Joseph has dreams where his brothers' sheaves of grain bow down to his sheaf, and where the sun, moon, and eleven stars bow down to him. He naively tells his brothers these dreams. They hate him even more.

58/95

Dreams That Point to God's Plan

Joseph's dreams weren't just about his future greatness - they were prophetic visions of how God would use him to save many lives. But Joseph doesn't understand this yet, and neither do his brothers.

Sometimes God's plans for us seem impossible or even create conflict, but He's always working for a greater purpose.

59/95

Betrayed by Brothers

One day, Israel sends Joseph to check on his brothers who are grazing flocks far from home. When they see him coming, they plot to kill him. "Here comes that dreamer!" they say. Reuben convinces them not to kill him, so instead they throw him in a cistern.

When Midianite merchants pass by, the brothers sell Joseph as a slave for twenty shekels of silver. They dip his special robe in goat's blood and tell their father that Joseph was killed by a wild animal. Israel is devastated.

60/95

From Slave to Overseer

Joseph is sold to Potiphar, captain of Pharaoh's guard in Egypt. But "the Lord was with Joseph" and he prospered. Potiphar notices that everything Joseph touches succeeds, so he puts Joseph in charge of his entire household.

But then Potiphar's wife notices how handsome Joseph is. Day after day, she tries to seduce him. Joseph refuses: "How could I do such a wicked thing and sin against God?" When she grabs his cloak, he runs away, leaving the garment in her hands.

61/95

From Overseer to Prisoner

Potiphar's wife falsely accuses Joseph of assault. He's thrown into prison - specifically, the prison where the king's prisoners are kept. This seems like the end for Joseph. Sold by his brothers, falsely accused, now rotting in an Egyptian dungeon.

But again, "the Lord was with him." The warden puts Joseph in charge of all the prisoners. God is positioning Joseph exactly where he needs to be.

62/95

The Gift of Dream Interpretation

Pharaoh's cupbearer and baker are thrown into prison. Both have troubling dreams the same night. Joseph interprets them: the cupbearer will be restored to his position in three days, but the baker will be executed. Everything happens exactly as Joseph said.

Joseph asks the cupbearer to remember him when he's restored, but the cupbearer forgets all about Joseph. Two more years pass with Joseph in prison. It seems like God has forgotten him too.

63/95

Then Pharaoh has two disturbing dreams that none of his wise men can interpret. Finally, the cupbearer remembers Joseph! Joseph is quickly brought from the dungeon, cleaned up, and brought before Pharaoh.

Joseph interprets the dreams: seven years of abundance followed by seven years of severe famine. He advises Pharaoh to store grain during the good years. Pharaoh is so impressed that he makes Joseph second-in-command over all Egypt! In one day, Joseph goes from prisoner to prime minister!

64/95

The Dreams Come True

The famine hits, affecting even Canaan where Joseph's family lives. His brothers come to Egypt to buy grain and bow down before the Egyptian official in charge - not recognizing it's Joseph! The dreams from twenty years earlier are literally coming true.

Joseph tests his brothers to see if they've changed, then dramatically reveals himself. They're terrified, but Joseph weeps and embraces them.

65/95

God's Amazing Plan

"You intended to harm me, but God intended it for good to accomplish what is now being done, the saving of many lives."
- Genesis 50:20

Joseph invites his entire family - 70 people total - to move to Egypt where he can provide for them. What seemed like disaster after disaster was actually God positioning His people exactly where they needed to be.

üåä Moses and the Exodus

From Slavery to Freedom

68/95

From Guests to Slaves ‚õìÔ∏è

Joseph's family of 70 grows into a nation of perhaps 2 million. But a new Pharaoh arises "who did not know about Joseph." Fearing the Israelites' numbers, he enslaves them. For 400 years, they suffer under brutal oppression.

Pharaoh even orders all Hebrew baby boys to be thrown into the Nile River. God's chosen people are facing genocide. They cry out to God in their misery.

69/95

A Baby in a Basket

One Hebrew mother, Jochebed, hides her baby boy for three months. When she can't hide him anymore, she waterproofs a basket and places him among the reeds of the Nile.

Pharaoh's daughter finds the basket while bathing. The baby cries, and her heart melts. She adopts him and names him Moses, which means "drawn out of the water." Ironically, the future deliverer of Israel is raised in Pharaoh's own household!

70/95

Prince to Fugitive

Moses grows up with the best education in Egypt, but he knows he's Hebrew. At age 40, he sees an Egyptian beating a Hebrew slave. In anger, Moses kills the Egyptian and hides the body. When Pharaoh finds out, Moses has to flee for his life.

For the next 40 years, Moses lives as a shepherd in Midian. The prince of Egypt becomes a nobody in the wilderness. It seems like his chance to help his people is gone forever.

71/95

The Burning Bush üî•

At age 80, when Moses thinks his life is basically over, he sees something strange: a bush on fire that isn't burning up. When he investigates, God speaks from the bush: "Moses! Moses! Take off your sandals, for the place where you are standing is holy ground."

"I have indeed seen the misery of my people in Egypt. I have heard them crying out... So I have come down to rescue them... So now, go. I am sending you to Pharaoh."
- Exodus 3:7-10
72/95

God "Remembers" His Covenant üß†

"God heard their groaning and he remembered his covenant with Abraham, with Isaac and with Jacob."
- Exodus 2:24

Remember, when the Bible says God "remembers," it doesn't mean He forgot. It means He's about to act on His promises. 400 years of silence, but God was preparing the perfect moment for deliverance.

73/95

"Let My People Go!"

Moses (now 80) and his brother Aaron confront Pharaoh: "This is what the Lord, the God of Israel, says: 'Let my people go.'" Pharaoh's response? "Who is the Lord, that I should obey him? I do not know the Lord and I will not let Israel go."

Pharaoh not only refuses but makes the Israelites' work harder. They now have to gather their own straw to make bricks while maintaining the same quota. The people turn against Moses - his "help" has made things worse!

74/95

The Battle of the Gods

What follows is not just a power struggle between Moses and Pharaoh. Each plague is a direct attack on Egypt's gods, showing that the Lord is supreme over all!

Through ten devastating plagues - from turning the Nile to blood to three days of darkness - God demonstrates His power. Each time, Pharaoh promises to let them go, then hardens his heart.

Finally, God announces the most terrible plague of all...

75/95

The Passover ü©∏

God announces the final plague: the death of every firstborn in Egypt. But He provides a way of escape for His people. Each family must sacrifice a perfect lamb and put its blood on their doorframes. When the destroyer sees the blood, he will "pass over" that house.

Do you see Jesus here? A perfect lamb dies so that death passes over God's people. This is why Jesus is called "the Lamb of God"!

76/95

The Passover Lamb Requirements

Notice the specific requirements for the Passover lamb:

  • Must be without defect (perfect)
  • A year old male (in its prime)
  • Must be examined for 4 days
  • No bones could be broken
  • Blood must be applied

Every detail points to Jesus - examined for days, crucified at twilight, no bones broken!

77/95

Free at Last!

That terrible night, Pharaoh's own son dies. Finally broken, he not only lets Israel go but begs them to leave. The Egyptians give them gold, silver, and clothing - 400 years of back wages! About 600,000 men plus women and children leave Egypt.

But Pharaoh changes his mind one more time and pursues them with his army...

78/95

God Parts the Waters! üåä

Trapped between Pharaoh's army and the Red Sea, the Israelites panic. But Moses says, "Stand firm and you will see the deliverance the Lord will bring you today."

Moses stretches out his staff, and God drives the sea back with a strong east wind all night. The waters divide, creating walls of water on both sides. The Israelites walk through on dry ground!

When the Egyptians pursue, the waters return and Pharaoh's entire army drowns. God fights for His people!

85/95

Spying Out the Promised Land

After leaving Mount Sinai, they finally reach the edge of the Promised Land - the land God promised to Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob. Moses sends twelve spies (one from each tribe) to scout out the land.

After 40 days, the spies return with a cluster of grapes so large it takes two men to carry it! The land truly flows with milk and honey, just as God promised. But then comes the bad news...

86/95

Giants in the Land! üò±

Ten of the spies give a terrifying report: "The people who live there are powerful, and the cities are fortified and very large. We even saw descendants of Anak there... We seemed like grasshoppers in our own eyes, and we looked the same to them."

But two spies - Joshua and Caleb - have a different perspective: "The land we passed through is exceedingly good. If the Lord delights in us, He will bring us into this land and give it to us!"

87/95

Choosing Fear Over Faith

The people side with the ten fearful spies. They weep all night, grumbling against Moses and Aaron: "If only we had died in Egypt! Or in this wilderness! Why is the Lord bringing us to this land only to let us fall by the sword?"

They even talk about choosing a new leader and going back to Egypt - back to slavery! After all God has done - the plagues, the Passover, parting the Red Sea, providing manna - they still don't trust Him.

88/95

The Consequence of Unbelief

"Not one of you will enter the land I swore with uplifted hand to make your home, except Caleb son of Jephunneh and Joshua son of Nun."
- Numbers 14:30

God declares that this entire generation will wander in the wilderness for 40 years - one year for each day the spies were in the land. Everyone 20 years old and older will die in the wilderness, except Joshua and Caleb who trusted God.

89/95

A Generation Dies in the Desert

For 40 long years, they wander in circles in the wilderness. God still provides manna every morning. Their clothes and sandals don't wear out. He leads them with a pillar of cloud by day and fire by night. His mercy continues even in judgment.

One by one, the generation that saw God's mighty works in Egypt dies off. Even Moses' sister Miriam and brother Aaron die in the wilderness. An entire generation perishes because of unbelief.

90/95

The Bronze Snake: Another Picture of Jesus! üêç

When the people complain against God, venomous snakes bite them and many die. They cry out for help, and God tells Moses to make a bronze snake and put it on a pole. Anyone who looks at it will live.

"Just as Moses lifted up the snake in the wilderness, so the Son of Man must be lifted up, that everyone who believes may have eternal life in him."
- John 3:14-15

Jesus Himself said this pointed to His crucifixion - look and live!

91/95

Even Moses Falls Short

Near the end of the 40 years, the people complain about water again. God tells Moses to speak to a rock and water will come out. But Moses is angry with the people. Instead of speaking to the rock, he strikes it twice with his staff.

Water gushes out, but God says to Moses: "Because you did not trust in me enough to honor me as holy in the sight of the Israelites, you will not bring this community into the land I give them."

Even the greatest prophet of the Old Testament couldn't earn his way into the Promised Land!
