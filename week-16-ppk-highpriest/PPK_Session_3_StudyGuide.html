OUR GREAT HIGH PRIEST

▶ WATCH VIDEO
Note: This session combines portions of two related messages for a cohesive study on Jesus as our Great High Priest.

Summary

This session explores Jesus as our Great High Priest, examining what priests did in the Old Testament and how Jesus perfectly fulfills and surpasses that role. We'll discover how the priesthood began in the Garden of Eden, was formalized under Moses, and ultimately points to Jesus who offered himself as both priest and sacrifice. Most importantly, we'll see how Jesus' priestly work means we can now approach God directly and confidently—and that every believer is now part of God's priesthood.

Highlight Sentence

Jesus is our Great High Priest who offered himself as the ultimate sacrifice, satisfying God's justice and removing our sin completely, so that we can now enter God's presence with confidence and become part of his priesthood forever.

Main Points

  • A priest mediates between God and people—representing them, preparing them, and going before them into God's presence.
  • Priesthoods appear in nearly every culture throughout history, revealing humanity's universal awareness of sin, our lack of merit to deal with it ourselves, and the need for substitutionary sacrifice.
  • The priesthood concept began in the Garden of Eden when God covered Adam and Eve with animal skins—the first substitutionary sacrifice.
  • Old Testament priests had two key qualifications: they had to be appointed by God and they had to be holy (set apart).
  • Jesus perfectly fulfilled both qualifications—appointed by God as the eternal priest-king in the order of Melchizedek, and completely holy through his perfect obedience to the Father.
  • The two essential functions of priests were: (1) leading worship through sacrifices that made it possible to approach God, and (2) interceding on behalf of the people.
  • On the Day of Atonement, two goats were used: one was sacrificed to satisfy God's justice (propitiation), and one was sent away carrying the people's sin-guilt (expiation). Jesus fulfilled both roles—he satisfied the Father's justice through his death AND carried our sin away completely.
  • Jesus' priestly work continues in heaven—not by begging the Father on our behalf, but through his resurrected presence at God's right hand, bearing the scars that prove "it is finished."

1. INTRODUCTION (48:16)
1.1 Series Overview: Jesus Christ - Prophet, Priest and King
1.1.1 Two weeks ago we began a series entitled Jesus Christ: Prophet, Priest and King.
1.1.2 These three divinely instituted offices were first revealed in the Old Testament as ways God related to the ancient Israelites.
1.1.3 Each office was anointed by God and fulfilled a specific relational purpose:
(a) Through the prophet God spoke to his people.
(b) Through the priest God brought his people to himself.
(c) Through the king God protected his people.
1.2 Jesus Fulfills All Three Offices
1.2.1 Ultimately these offices foreshadowed a superior and final Anointed One who embodied all three offices: Prophet, Priest and King—the Messiah revealed in the New Testament as Jesus Christ.
1.2.2 Everything Jesus did before his incarnation (as revealed in the Old Testament), during his earthly ministry (the Gospels), and now in his heavenly ministry (the Epistles) he did as Prophet, Priest and King.
1.2.3 Therefore, part of coming to know and trust Jesus is knowing him as Prophet, Priest and King.
1.3 Focus on High Priest
1.3.1 Last week we looked at Jesus the Ultimate Prophet.
1.3.2 This week we begin to look at Jesus the Superior High Priest, the predominant theme of Hebrews.
1.3.3 The author summarizes in Hebrews 8:1: "Now the main point of what we are saying is this: We do have such a high priest."
2. WHAT IS A PRIEST? (48:54)
2.1 Basic Definition
2.1.1 A priest mediates between God and humanity—particularly between God and his people—so that God will receive them into his holy presence and bless them.
2.1.2 The priest represents the people, prepares them, and goes before them.
2.1.3 In the Bible, representation, preparation and going before the people is the job of the priest.
2.2 Study Plan
2.2.1 This morning and next week we will examine:
(a) What a priest was under the Old Covenant
(b) How Jesus fulfills the Old Covenant priesthood
(c) How Jesus is our Priest under the New Covenant
(d) How Jesus' priesthood impacts our lives

3.1 Universal Presence of Priesthoods
3.1.1 Priesthoods appear in nearly every society in human history.
3.1.2 This reveals three important truths:
3.2 First Revelation: Universal Consciousness of Sin
3.2.1 Romans 2 indicates people have an inner awareness of their sin even without God's written law.
3.2.2 In nearly every religion this has given rise to some kind of priesthood to represent them before their god.
3.3 Second Revelation: Humans Feel Without Merit
3.3.1 Humans feel they lack merit to atone for their own sin.

4.1 God's Warning to Adam
4.1.1 Before the Fall, God told Adam that eating from the tree of the knowledge of good and evil would result in death.
4.1.2 Adam and Eve disobeyed yet did not immediately die.

4.3.2 But it began with the One True God in the Garden.

5.2 The Gospel's Good News

6. THE OLD TESTAMENT PRIESTHOOD (50:02)
6.1 Priesthood Before Moses
6.1.1 When Christians think about priesthood they usually think of the Mosaic priesthood, but the priesthood existed long before Moses.
6.1.2 Genesis 1-2 indicate Adam was to rule as king and function as a priest, tending the earth to make it suitable for God's presence.
6.1.3 After the flood, Noah acted as priest for his family.
6.1.4 Job functions as priest in his household.
6.1.5 Abraham encounters Melchizedek, king and priest of Salem.
6.1.6 Exodus 3 records Jethro as God's priest in Midian.
6.2 Official Priesthood Established
6.2.1 These early priests appear without continuity until Moses.
6.2.2 Through Moses, God established an official, exclusive priesthood: Aaron and his descendants from the tribe of Levi.
6.3 Both Point to Jesus
6.3.1 Thus the Old Testament shows an unofficial priesthood followed by the Levitical priesthood.
6.3.2 Both foreshadowed the ultimate priest, Jesus Christ.
6.3.3 To understand Jesus' priestly ministry we must grasp key qualifications and functions of the Old Testament priesthood.
7. QUALIFICATIONS OF THE PRIESTHOOD (52:33)
7.1 Two Key Requirements
7.1.1 The Old Testament emphasizes two qualifications:
(a) Priests must be appointed by God
(b) They must be holy (set apart) before God
8. QUALIFICATION 1: PRIESTS WERE APPOINTED BY GOD
8.1 Divine Appointment Only
8.1.1 Priests were never self-appointed.
8.1.2 They were not chosen by other priests, kings, or the people.
8.1.3 Only God appoints priests.
8.2 God's Command to Moses
8.2.1 Exodus 28 commands Moses to have Aaron and his sons brought so they may serve as priests.
8.2.2 Later, Numbers makes the calling serious: anyone who presumed to do priestly work outside Aaron's lineage faced death.
8.3 Exclusive to Aaron's Line
8.3.1 The Mosaic priesthood was therefore exclusive to Aaron's family in the tribe of Levi.
8.4 Confirmed in Hebrews
8.4.1 Hebrews 5 echoes this: "Every high priest is selected from among the people and is appointed to represent the people... And no one takes this honor on himself, but he receives it when called by God, just as Aaron was."
9. QUALIFICATION 2: PRIESTS WERE HOLY (SET APART)
9.1 Required Holiness
9.1.1 Because priests served near God's special presence in the tabernacle and temple, they needed to be holy.
9.1.2 By holy we mean not only morally but in being set apart.
9.2 Regulations Emphasized Separation
9.2.1 The many regulations and ritual cleansings governed their lives and emphasized their separation for God's service.
9.2.2 God's point was that these men were uniquely set apart.
9.2.3 Access to God's presence required them.
10. JESUS PERFECTLY FULFILLS THE QUALIFICATIONS (55:46)
11. JESUS WAS APPOINTED
11.1 Divine Appointment Confirmed
11.1.1 Hebrews 5 reiterates that no one takes the high priesthood on himself but is called by God.
11.1.2 In the same way, Christ did not assume the glory of becoming high priest.
11.1.3 God declared, "You are my Son" and elsewhere "You are a priest forever, in the order of Melchizedek" (Hebrews 5:1,4-6).
11.2 Anointing at Baptism
11.2.1 Jesus' anointing at his baptism marks his divine appointment as Prophet, Priest and King.
11.2.2 When the Father said, "This is my Son; listen to him"—this was his official appointment.
11.2.3 This appointing took place in the Jordan River when Jesus was baptized by John and anointed with the Holy Spirit for his priestly ministry.
11.3 Not Levitical but Melchizedek
11.3.1 Although Jesus is from Judah, not Levi, he fulfills the priesthood in the order of Melchizedek: a king-priest.
11.3.2 But he was not appointed and anointed as simply another Levitical priest in the order of Aaron like all other priests of Israel.
11.3.3 He was appointed as priest forever in the order of Melchizedek which identified Jesus as the Promised Messiah.
11.3.4 According to Psalm 110, he would be both High Priest who served forever and King who ruled forever.
11.3.5 This is exactly what God promised in 1 Samuel 2.
11.4 Significance of King-Priest
11.4.1 Hebrews 7 argues that this shows:
(a) Jesus is the promised Messiah, the king-priest
(b) Salvation comes not through the Levitical law but through the priesthood and grace of King Jesus (cf. John 1:17)
11.5 Connection to Adam
11.5.1 Other than Melchizedek and Jesus, the only other priest-king in Scripture was Adam.
11.5.2 The first Adam failed.
11.5.3 The second Adam (Christ) succeeded by obeying the Father in the second garden, saying, "Not my will but your will be done."
11.5.4 Because of that obedience we have salvation.

12.1.3 He was set apart or holy to the Lord.
12.2 Scripture Confirms His Holiness

12.4 No Need for His Own Sacrifice

12.5.1 Through his obedience we are saved.
12.5.2 His righteousness becomes ours (cf. 2 Corinthians 5:21).

14. FUNCTION 1: THE PRIESTS LED WORSHIP (1:03:02)
14.1 Making Worship Possible

15. THE DAY OF ATONEMENT (YOM KIPPUR)
15.1 Background and Setting
15.1.1 The penalty for sin is death—someone must die.
15.1.2 Because of our sin, all human beings deserve divine judgment.
15.1.3 In the garden of Eden God said, "If you eat of that fruit you shall surely die" (Genesis 2:17).
15.1.4 In Romans 6: "The wages of sin is death" (Romans 6:23).
15.2 God's Way of Escape
15.2.1 In spite of this crushing reality God has provided a way of escape through substitutionary sacrifice.
15.2.2 We first see it in the Garden when the life of an animal was taken to provide coverings for Adam and Eve.
15.2.3 We see it again on the night of the Passover when the life blood of a lamb covered the people from divine judgment.
15.2.4 Finally we see substitutionary sacrifice codified in the law of Moses God through the various animal sacrifices offered by the priests.
15.3 Temporary Nature of Old Testament Sacrifices
15.3.1 But none of these sacrifices throughout the Old Testament provided permanent forgiveness of sin.
15.3.2 Yes, through them God forgave his people but not on the basis of the value or merit of the offering itself.
15.3.3 Rather on what they pointed forward to, the ultimate final sacrifice—the sacrifice of Jesus revealed in the New Testament.
15.4 Annual Renewal Required
15.4.1 Since these sacrifices in the Old Testament were temporary in nature they needed to be renewed over and over each year on the Day of Atonement (Yom Kippur).
15.5 The Tabernacle Structure
15.5.1 From the days of Moses to David this took place in the Tabernacle.
15.5.2 The Tabernacle was a three-part, tent-like structure:
(a) A fenced off courtyard
(b) A large covered room called the holy place
(c) In the rear of that large room a small room cordoned off with a very thick curtain called the most holy place
16. THE TWO GOATS CEREMONY
16.1 Preparation
16.1.1 On the Day of Atonement, after the high priest offered a bull for his own sin and that of his household, there was brought before him two goats.
16.1.2 These goats had to be as similar in appearance as possible.
16.1.3 They also had to be unblemished to represent sinlessness.
16.1.4 Just like the original lambs that were offered on the night of the first Passover in Egypt.
16.1.5 Then lots were cast to determined what happened next to each goat.
16.2 First Goat: The Sacrifice (Propitiation)
16.2.1 One goat was slain.
16.2.2 Its blood was carried by the High Priest past the thick curtain into the most holy place of the Tabernacle/Temple.
16.2.3 He sprinkled it over the mercy seat (Hebrews 9:7).
16.3 The Mercy Seat Explained
16.3.1 The mercy seat was a lid of a gold covered wood box called the ark of the covenant.
16.3.2 Above the mercy seat was the manifested presence of God.
16.3.3 Below it were the stone tablets of the law of Moses.
16.4 What the Blood Accomplished
16.4.1 The sprinkled blood covered over the law which the people had broken.
16.4.2 So that when God looked down their sins were covered or atoned for.
16.4.3 That was atonement.
16.5 Propitiation Defined
16.5.1 But this offering of blood on the mercy seat did something else.
16.5.2 It satisfied the demand of God's justice that the sin be judged.
16.5.3 Instead of the people being judged the animal was judged as their substitute.
16.5.4 Now God is free to bless instead of judge the sinner because their sin had been judged in a substitute.
16.5.5 That's called propitiation.
16.5.6 It's a lamb for God in that he came to satisfy God's justice.
16.6 Second Goat: The Scapegoat (Expiation)
16.6.1 Then the High Priest would come back out into the courtyard.
16.6.2 He would lay his hands on the remaining goat to symbolically transfer the people's sin-guilt to the goat.
16.6.3 After which it was then taken outside the camp into the wilderness to die as the scapegoat (Hebrews 13:12).
16.7 Expiation Defined
16.7.1 This represented the carrying away or removal of the people's sin-guilt.
16.7.2 Now the sinner is free from their sin and able to enter God's presence.
16.7.3 Because their sin had been carried away by a substitute.
16.7.4 That's called expiation.
16.7.5 It's a lamb for us in that he takes away our sin like the scapegoat.

17. JESUS FULFILLS THE DAY OF ATONEMENT
17.1 Why Jesus Came
17.1.1 This is why the Son of God, Jesus Christ came into the world through the incarnation.
17.1.2 "For this reason he had to be made like them, fully human in every way, in order that he might become a merciful and faithful high priest in service to God, and that he might make atonement for the sins of the people" (Hebrews 2:17).
17.2 Jesus as Both Priest and Sacrifice
17.2.1 Without a doubt, Jesus' death on the cross was the greatest way Jesus functioned in his priestly ministry.
17.2.2 It was also unique in the fact that Jesus not only made the offering for our sin, he also became that offering for our sin.
17.2.3 He was not only the ultimate priest who made the offering, he was the ultimate, once and for all, final sacrifice, the lamb of God.
17.3 Old Testament Priests vs. Jesus
17.3.1 The Old Testament priests were responsible for continually offering a variety of offerings and sacrifices on behalf of the people.
17.3.2 Over the centuries there were millions of bulls, lambs and goats offered.
17.3.3 None of them permanently took away sin.
17.3.4 None of them permanently satisfied the justice of God.
17.3.5 But every one of them foreshadowed the ultimate sacrificial offering that Jesus rendered when he died on the cross.
17.4 John's Declaration
17.4.1 One day as John was baptizing at the Jordan River he looked up and saw Jesus.
17.4.2 He said, "Behold the Lamb of God who takes away the sin of the world" (John 1:29).
17.5 Three Aspects of the Lamb
17.5.1 He is firstly a Lamb from God.
(a) He is God's provision to bear the judgment for our sin.
(b) For only God could bear the wrath of God.
17.5.2 He is secondly a Lamb for God.
(a) In that he came to satisfy God's justice or propitiate God.
17.5.3 He is thirdly a Lamb for us.
(a) In that he takes away our sin like the scapegoat (expiation).
17.6 What "God So Loved" Means
17.6.1 When the Bible says "God so loved the world he gave his one and only son" this is exactly what it means.
17.6.2 God loved us so much He gave up his own Son as a sacrifice on the cross.
17.6.3 In order to propitiate or satisfy the demands of his own justice.
17.6.4 That's why Jesus said, "It is finished."
17.6.5 Why? The Father is satisfied.
17.7 No More Condemnation
17.7.1 This is also why in Romans 8 "There is no more condemnation for those who are in Christ Jesus" (Romans 8:1).
17.7.2 Jesus satisfied the justice of God by taking our judgment upon himself.
17.7.3 He paid the penalty in full.
17.8 Propitiation for All
17.8.1 In 1 John 2 the Apostle John says:
17.8.2 "My little children, I am writing these things to you so that you may not sin. But if anyone does sin, we have an advocate with the Father, Jesus Christ the righteous. He is the propitiation for our sins, and not for ours only but also for the sins of the whole world" (1 John 2:1-2 ESV).
17.9 Expiation - Removal of Sin-Guilt
17.9.1 But Jesus not only provided propitiation he also provided expiation or removal of sin-guilt from us.
17.9.2 He is not only seen in the goat that was slain at the altar of the temple.
17.9.3 He is also the goat that was taken to the wilderness to die.
17.9.4 He bears our sin-penalty and removes our sin-guilt.
17.10 One Story, One Hero
17.10.1 That is why Jesus, like the scapegoat, was taken outside the city to be crucified.
17.10.2 Because Jesus also removes sin-guilt (like the scapegoat), he was crucified outside the city.
17.10.3 One story, one hero: Jesus bore our penalty and carried our guilt away.

18.1 Question for Those Who Doubt God's Forgiveness
18.1.1 Now in the light of such a Great High Priest how can we ever say "I don't know if God can forgive me? I don't know if God will forgive me"?
18.1.2 The only reason you would say that is that you have yet to take him as your Lamb and Priest.
18.1.3 Take him as your high priest. Take him as your lamb.
18.1.4 He satisfied the Father. He took away your sin.
18.2 Question for Those Who Can't Forgive Themselves

18.2.2 Why would you need to forgive yourself with such a Great High Priest forgiving you?
18.2.3 In essence that is saying "I need something more than this Great High Priest in order to be whole."
18.2.4 Why would you need to forgive yourself if He is your lamb of God?
18.2.5 Why would you need to carry your sins outside the city if he carried them away for you?
18.2.6 By faith, take him as your High Priest who gave himself for you.

19. FUNCTION 2: INTERCESSION CONTINUES IN HEAVEN
19.1 Hebrews: Permanent Priesthood
19.1.1 Because "The Lord has sworn... 'You are a priest forever,'" Jesus is the guarantor of a better covenant (Hebrews 7:21-25).
19.1.2 He lives forever and therefore has a permanent priesthood.
19.2 Complete Salvation
19.2.1 He is able to save completely those who come to God through Him.
19.2.2 Because He always lives to intercede for them.
19.3 Jesus' Priestly Ministry Continues Now
19.3.1 On earth—He interceded by prayer.
19.3.2 On the cross—He interceded by sacrifice.
19.3.3 In heaven—He intercedes now by living for us (His resurrected presence at the Father's right hand).
19.4 Romans Affirms This
19.4.1 Romans 8:33-34: "Who will bring a charge against God's elect? It is God who justifies. Who is to condemn? Christ Jesus... is at the right hand of God and is also interceding for us."
20. WHAT "INTERCEDING" MEANS
20.1 Common Misunderstanding
20.1.1 Many assume these verses mean Jesus is verbally praying for you in heaven.
20.1.2 The texts say He intercedes—which is broader than "is praying."
20.2 Broader Definition
20.2.1 Prayer is one means of intercession.
20.2.2 But intercession broadly can be action on behalf of someone—mediation, advocacy, representation.
20.3 How Jesus Intercedes
20.3.1 The writer argues Jesus intercedes by His resurrected, ascended presence at the Father's right hand.
20.3.2 Not by continually begging the Father to be satisfied.
20.3.3 Scripture repeatedly says Jesus is seated.
20.4 Picture of Intercession
20.4.1 Personification: imagine the Father looking to the armrest.
20.4.2 Seeing the nail-pierced hands of His Son.
20.4.3 And nodding in affirmation: "It is finished."
20.4.4 Nothing more needs to be added.

21.1.4 Was his blood not enough?
21.1.5 Is the Father somehow not favorably disposed to us?
21.1.6 Does Jesus need to convince him to help us?
21.2 Questions About Our Standing
21.2.1 Was our redemption price too small?
21.2.2 Has our justification been revoked?
21.2.3 Have our adoption papers been lost?
21.2.4 Has our heavenly inheritance been amended?
21.3 The Cross is Sufficient
21.3.1 No, the cross is sufficient.
21.3.2 The work of salvation is finished and there is nothing else to be done or added to it.
21.3.3 There are no more prayers to be made.
21.4 "It Is Finished" (Tetelestai)
21.4.1 "It is finished" (tetelestai).
21.4.2 The work has been completed with results that remain finished forever.
21.4.3 The cross is sufficient: redemption stands, justification is not revoked, adoption papers are not lost.
21.5 Physical Presence, Not Verbal Prayers
21.5.1 Jesus intercedes not primarily by adding words.
21.5.2 But by His physical, resurrected, ascended, and eternal presence.
21.5.3 His "scarred hands and side presence" in heaven as our Great High Priest.
21.5.4 Bearing the scars that prove "it is finished."
22. SECURITY OF SALVATION
22.1 Permanent Security
22.1.1 Because Jesus lives forever and intercedes forever as the eternal high priest, our salvation is permanently secure.
22.1.2 For our salvation to fail the high priest would have to die.
22.1.3 But he cannot die for he is our priest forever.
22.1.4 Because our Great high Priest Jesus lives and intercedes forever as the ultimate mediator our salvation is permanently secure.
22.2 Scripture Support
22.2.1 "Because Jesus lives forever, he has a permanent priesthood" (Hebrews 7:24).
23. THE PRIESTHOOD BELONGS TO BELIEVERS
23.1 Important Nuance in Hebrews 7:24
23.1.1 Notice it does not say because Jesus lives forever he is a permanent priest.
23.1.2 That is true.
23.1.3 But Jesus is not only a permanent priest, he has a permanent priesthood.
23.1.4 And the Bible says that priesthood is made up of all who believe in him.
23.2 God's Original Design - Exodus 19
23.2.1 From the very beginning that has been God's design.
23.2.2 At Sinai God said He desired Israel to be "a kingdom of priests" if they would obey His covenant.
23.2.3 "You yourselves have seen what I did to Egypt, and how I carried you on eagles' wings and brought you to myself. Now if you obey me fully and keep my covenant, then out of all nations you will be my treasured possession. Although the whole earth is mine, you will be for me a kingdom of priests and a holy nation" (Exodus 19:4-6).
23.2.4 From the beginning God's design was inclusive priesthood for His people.
23.3 Historical Failure
23.3.1 So what God was saying was that He wanted all of them to be a priesthood.
23.3.2 But they couldn't because of their sin.
23.3.3 They couldn't go up the mountain of God and stand in the presence of God.
23.4 God's Remedy Through Mediators
23.4.1 So the people chose Moses to approach God for them.
23.4.2 Basically, they said "Moses you go up the mountain for us. Be our priest."
23.4.3 Not long afterwards, God tells Moses to consecrate the sons of Aaron, the Levites, to serve as priests for the people.
23.4.4 Israel could not fully be that priesthood because of sin.
23.4.5 They chose Moses and later the Aaronic priests as representatives.
23.5 God's Desire Remained
23.5.1 But God's desire remained.
23.5.2 Generations later, the prophet like Moses, the priest like Melchizedek and the King like David came into the world.
23.5.3 To form the priesthood God desired from the very beginning.
24. JESUS MAKES PRIESTHOOD POSSIBLE FOR BELIEVERS
24.1 Qualifications Made Possible
24.1.1 Jesus came to make it possible for us to meet the qualifications of the priesthood.
24.1.2 To be appointed and holy.
24.2 Duties Made Possible
24.2.1 And Jesus came to make it possible for us to fulfill the duties of the priesthood.
24.2.2 To worship, offer sacrifices and intercede.
25. PETER'S LANGUAGE (1 PETER 2:4, 9)
25.1 Qualifications and Duties Matrix
Qualifications:
- Appointed by God
- Holy to God
Duties:
- Worship
- Offerings (spiritual sacrifices)
- Intercession (declaring God's praises)
25.2 1 Peter 2:4,9
25.2.1 "As you come to him, the living Stone—rejected by humans but chosen by God and precious to him— you also, like living stones, are being built into a spiritual house to be a holy priesthood, offering spiritual sacrifices acceptable to God through Jesus Christ."
25.2.2 "But you are a chosen people, a royal priesthood, a holy nation, God's special possession, that you may declare the praises of him who called you out of darkness into his wonderful light" (1 Peter 2:4,9).
25.3 Peter Uses Similar Language to Exodus 19
25.3.1 In language very similar to Exodus 19 Peter writes about our priesthood in Jesus.
25.3.2 How God qualifies us for the new covenant priesthood.
25.3.3 And how we fulfill the duties of our priesthood.
26. HOW "DECLARING HIS PRAISE" IS PRIESTLY INTERCESSION
26.1 What It Means
26.1.1 Declaring his praise means proclaiming the excellencies of God.
26.1.2 The idea is not praising God but declaring his praise or the things he should be praised for.
26.1.3 "Proclaim the excellencies" (ESV, NASB).
26.2 Explaining to Others
26.2.1 It's saying to those in darkness let me explain to you why I praise God.
26.2.2 Through the excellencies of the gospel, Jesus' death and resurrection, he has brought me out of darkness into his wonderful light.
26.3 Greatest Form of Intercession
26.3.1 This declaration is priestly intercession.
26.3.2 Standing between people and Christ by pointing them to Jesus and the gospel (the greatest kind of intercession).
26.3.3 And that declaration is the greatest kind of priestly intercession.
26.4 Active Mediation
26.4.1 It is standing between them and Jesus and pointing them to Jesus.
26.4.2 It's interceding in their life in the greatest way.
26.4.3 It is an active mediation.
26.4.4 It's saying "Here is who Jesus is and what he has done, put your trust in him and join his priesthood."
27. CONCLUSION (APPLICATION & EXHORTATION)
28. BEING PART OF CHRIST'S PRIESTHOOD CHANGES YOUR RELATIONSHIP WITH GOD
28.1 Access, Closeness, and Intimacy
28.1.1 Being part of Christ's priesthood completely changes the way you relate to God.
28.1.2 As a priest you have access, closeness, and intimacy with God.
28.1.3 Since you're a priest you have access and access means closeness and intimacy with God.
28.2 Saved But Not Close
28.2.1 It is possible to be saved yet not experience closeness.
28.2.2 It is possible and it's sometimes the case that a person can be a thoroughly saved Christian and yet have no experiential closeness with God.
28.2.3 Due to pride or ignorance of our blood-bought priesthood.
28.3 Causes of Distance
28.3.1 This can be caused by several things.
28.3.2 Sometimes by pride that refuses to come before God like a little child.
28.3.3 But also an ignorance of our blood-bought priesthood.
28.4 Serving Without Closeness
28.4.1 You can serve God and be faithful to God and experience His faithfulness.
28.4.2 But until you see yourself as a priest in God's priesthood you will find it very difficult to be close to God.
28.5 Waiting vs. Going
28.5.1 Priests go to God; they do not wait just for God to come to them.
28.5.2 We are called to enter God's presence.
28.5.3 You will wait for Him to come to you but you will rarely go to Him.
28.5.4 And yet that is what priests do, they go to God.
28.5.5 They go into the garden, up the mountain, into the holy place.
28.6 Belief Determines Experience
28.6.1 Until you really believe that you by virtue of Christ's death and resurrection have been made a part of a holy priesthood where you have unhindered access there will always be some distance.
28.7 Looking for Other Priests
28.7.1 And therefore, you will always be more inclined to look for a priest of some sorts.
28.7.2 An intermediary, a go-between.
28.7.3 Someone who goes in for you.
28.7.4 A very spiritual spouse, parent, friend that will go in for you.
28.7.5 Get close for you, pray your prayers, sing your praises, lift their hands for you, experience the joy of the Lord for you.
28.8 God's Longing
28.8.1 And all the while God is longing for you to come in to his presence.
28.8.2 To come into his holy place.
28.8.3 To experience the joy of your priesthood.
