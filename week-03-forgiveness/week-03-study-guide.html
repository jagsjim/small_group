Forgiveness

Voddie Baucham

Watch on YouTube

Summary

Forgiveness means cancelling a debt by refusing to withhold attention, affection, or honor from someone who has wronged you, just as God in Christ cancelled your debt.

Main Points

1. Forgiveness is the one word that best captures what it means to be a Christian—it defines both our relationship with God and with each other.
2. Forgiveness means cancelling a debt, not just avoiding revenge. When someone destroys your phone, forgiveness means they don't have to buy you a new one.
3. We punish each other by withholding attention, affection, or honor. This is how we make people "pay" in relationships—the silent treatment, coldness, or refusing to show respect.
4. Forgiveness doesn't mean forgetting. Human beings weren't made to forget, and the power of forgiveness is in choosing not to punish despite remembering.
5. Greater intimacy requires greater forgiveness. The closer we are to someone, the more we see their sin, which means we need to forgive more often, not that they need to sin less.
6. We forgive because we are forgiven. Recognizing that Christ died for our sins kills the hypocrisy of being upset with others for their failures.
7. Unforgiveness is costly in every way—emotionally draining, harmful to your relationship with God, and destructive to sound theology and compassion.
8. Forgiveness is different from reconciliation. You can forgive someone who never asks for it or who has died, but reconciliation requires both people.

Transcript (Outline Format)

Opening: The Importance of Forgiveness (2:42)

1.1 If I could only use one word to describe Christians and Christianity, it would be forgiveness. Grace and salvation would come to mind, but forgiveness encapsulates so much of what it means to be Christian—both in terms of our relationship to God and in terms of our relationship to one another.

1.2 We've looked at Ephesians 4:32 before, but I want to move from the mechanism of forgiveness to the motivation for forgiveness. After almost three decades of gospel ministry, you see the same things over and over again. You watch people go down the same roads repeatedly.

1.3 Ministry is not all about the pulpit—it's about the counseling room, the kitchen table, where you sit with people and say, "I know this road you're about to walk down. I've seen people walk down this road a thousand times before. I can tell you where this road ends."

1.4 If there's one road that derails Christians more than any other, it's the road of unforgiveness. You see people walk away from marriages, families, friendships, and even the church because of unforgiveness.

Reading the Text (9:04)

2.1 Let's look at Ephesians 4:25-32: "Therefore having put away falsehood, let each one of you speak the truth with his neighbor, for we are members one of another. Be angry and do not sin; do not let the sun go down on your anger, and give no opportunity to the devil. Let the thief no longer steal, but rather let him labor, doing honest work with his own hands, so that he may have something to share with anyone in need. Let no corrupting talk come out of your mouths, but only such as is good for building up, as fits the occasion, that it may give grace to those who hear. And do not grieve the Holy Spirit of God, by whom you were sealed for the day of redemption. Let all bitterness and wrath and anger and clamor and slander be put away from you, along with all malice. Be kind to one another, tenderhearted, forgiving one another, as God in Christ forgave you."

Defining Forgiveness (10:53)

3.1 Forgiveness means cancelling a debt. One of our biggest problems with forgiveness is we don't define it correctly. Forgiveness is more than just a statement or a lack of hostility.

3.2 The classic example: You borrow my phone and destroy it. Forgiveness is not me saying, "I forgive you, meaning I'm not going to do bad things to you right now, but you still need to buy me another phone." By definition, forgiveness means you don't have to buy me another phone because I cancel the debt that you owe.

3.3 This is huge. We think "I forgive you" means "I'm not going to give full vent to my anger, but you're still going to pay." That is not forgiveness. Forgiveness means the debt is cancelled.

How We Punish in Relationships (13:27)

4.1 How do we translate debt cancellation into interpersonal relationships? Here's where the rubber meets the road: The way we punish one another is by withholding attention, affection, or honor.

4.2 Withholding attention: "I'm angry with you. I don't want to see you right now. Get out of my face." That means I am punishing you by withholding my attention from you.

4.3 Withholding affection: "You can have my attention, but there will be no warmth from me. You will know by the stone-hard look on my face, by the coldness with which I deal with you, that I am withholding my affection."

4.4 Withholding honor: Classic example—"My parents weren't great parents. It's their birthday, I'm not going to call them because they don't deserve it. That's how I'm going to punish them."

4.5 These are the ways in interpersonal relationships that we make one another pay. If we define forgiveness wrongly, we say with our mouth "I forgive you" but we still withhold attention, affection, and honor, which means you say you're forgiven, but you're not.

Forgiveness Within the Body of Christ (24:01)

5.1 This text is about forgiveness within the parameters of the body of believers. The whole paragraph is about the church. Verse 25: "We are members of one another"—that's the church.

5.2 The key is found in verse 30: "Do not grieve the Holy Spirit by whom you were sealed for the day of redemption." Looking at verse 29: "Let no corrupting talk come out of your mouths"—literally acidic words that corrode like acid, words that tear down.

5.3 At the end of chapter 2, we see the Spirit building a structure—the body of believers who belong to Christ and belong to one another. We grieve the Spirit by using corrosive words that tear down what He's building.

5.4 This forgiveness happens within the context of kindness and tenderheartedness that we have toward one another within the body. It's part of a broader expression of our union and communion with one another.

Greater Intimacy Requires Greater Forgiveness (32:24)

6.1 One of the great ironies is this belief that true love ought to require less forgiveness. I wish this was just a problem in young marriages, but this foolishness lasts for decades.

6.2 "You would think that this sin would be gone by now. If you really loved me, you wouldn't keep sinning against me in the same ways." Actually, the opposite is true.

6.3 Let me turn it into a mathematical equation. Let's say you perceive my sin once per three interactions. If we only interact three times a month, you only perceive my sin once a month. But what if we interact three times a day? My rate of sin hasn't changed—we just spend more time together.

6.4 It's not that your spouse loves other people more than they love you—they just don't see them as much. When we're sharing life together, having more than three interactions a month, we're actually getting to see one another's sin.

6.5 Here's the way we normally think about it: "We're spending more time together, I'm seeing more of your sin than before, that means you have an obligation to sin less." Actually, it means I have an obligation to forgive more.

6.6 Intimate relationships don't give me the right to look at you and say that if this relationship is going to work, you have to sin less. Greater intimacy requires greater forgiveness.

Two Sides of Forgiveness (42:04)

7.1 "Forgiving one another as God in Christ forgave you"—there are two sides to this. We forgive because we are forgiven, and we can forgive because we are forgiven.

7.2 You are forgiven. This is crucial because it kills the hypocrisy. The hypocrisy is: "Everyone out there has a sin problem and they're getting on my nerves because they sin so much, but when you understand that you have been forgiven..."

7.3 You are not this pristine, sinless person going through life being bombarded with the sins of others. You are a sinner who has also had to be forgiven. This changes your perspective.

7.4 The Apostle Paul referred to himself as the chief of sinners, and he wrote two-thirds of the New Testament. You sin. You need forgiveness.

7.5 The individual who keeps cutting people off because everybody's bombarding them with sin doesn't see that their greatest sin is unforgiveness. The problem isn't that there are so many sinners out there, but that there's so little forgiveness in here.

7.6 The cure for that is the cross. Recognize that Jesus did not have to shed less blood for you. When you get exasperated with the sins of others, go back and visit the cross again and look into your Savior's face.

We Can Forgive Because We're Forgiven (46:11)

8.1 This changes not only the way I view my sin—it changes the way I view yours. Christ died for that.

8.2 Unforgiveness is sinister. It does a number of things:

8.3 Unforgiveness is costly emotionally. It costs me a lot emotionally to constantly punish you. I have to remember what you did, how much attention, affection, and honor I need to withhold until I feel like I've made you pay enough. That's draining.

8.4 It doesn't fix people. James tells us the anger of man does not produce the righteousness of God. I'm withholding from you because I want to inflict emotional damage so that next time you're afraid to do this again. I do not believe that Christ sanctifies—I believe my anger does.

8.5 It's costly in terms of your relationship with God. People who are unforgiving toward others always question their own salvation because they think God's forgiveness is the same as theirs. You withhold from people when they sin, and when you recognize sin in your own heart, you assume God will withhold from you.

8.6 It's costly in terms of your theology. Here's what you're saying: "That thing you did to me—the blood of Jesus is enough to satisfy the triune God, but I require more. The creator of the universe requires the death of his Son, but I have higher standards. I require you feeling alienated from me."

8.7 It will kill your compassion. Cultivating forgiveness creates compassion. I recognize I'm forgiven, and now you've sinned against me, and I recognize you need the forgiveness I've experienced. Instead of becoming embittered toward you, I'm driven to my knees on your behalf.

Application: Just Forgive (52:23)

9.1 You have been forgiven, so you can forgive. The forgiveness that Christ has purchased for you on the cross has transformed you, and you now have the ability to forgive.

9.2 Looking at Ephesians 2:13-18: "But now in Christ Jesus you who once were far off have been brought near by the blood of Christ. For he himself is our peace, who has made us both one and has broken down in his flesh the dividing wall of hostility... that he might reconcile us both to God in one body through the cross, thereby killing the hostility."

9.3 The cross has made peace between me and God, and in turn it has made peace between me and you. I am forgiven, therefore I can forgive. I can be reconciled to you because I've been reconciled to God through the cross of Jesus Christ.

9.4 Your sins, even the sins that you commit against me, are under the blood of Christ. What do I have left to hold on to?

Important Distinctions (54:58)

10.1 There is a difference between forgiveness and reconciliation. Reconciliation is a two-way street; forgiveness is a lonely highway. Some people say, "I would forgive, but they haven't asked for forgiveness." Not required.

10.2 You can forgive a dead person. Some of you need to because there's somebody who hurt you—a parent, grandparent—and they're not even alive anymore, and you're holding on to unforgiveness, destroying your own life. Holding on to unforgiveness is like drinking poison hoping they die.

10.3 Forgiveness does not negate our obligation to confront sin. "If your brother sins, rebuke him. If he repents, forgive him." We still do that, but there's a difference between bringing sin to someone's attention and withholding attention, affection, and honor so they feel punished.

10.4 Seek reconciliation, pray for reconciliation, be open to reconciliation, but while you're seeking and praying and being open—forgive. Don't withhold attention, don't withhold affection, don't withhold honor.

Final Applications (59:52)

11.1 Children, forgive. You wanted something, Mom and Dad said no, so you fold your arms and turn your head. What is that? Withholding attention, affection, and honor to punish Mom and Dad. That's unforgiveness.

11.2 Husbands and wives, forgive. Are you withholding attention, affection, honor from one another? I've seen it go on so long that couples can't even agree on what the original cause was.

11.3 Parents, forgive. Have your children sinned against you? That's a rhetorical question. Forgive. We don't use withholding attention, affection, and honor as punishment.

11.4 Church members, forgive. Has some member of your church, some leader, sinned against you? Forgive.

11.5 Don't let superficial forgiveness be window dressing. Like football players who get a pat on the back after a penalty but know they'll pay for it in tomorrow's film session—don't let that be your family, your marriage, your church.

11.6 Be kind to one another, tenderhearted, forgiving each other as God in Christ forgave you.

Final Appeal (1:04:43)

12.1 If you're here today and you have not experienced this forgiveness in Christ, then before you worry about forgiving others, recognize your desperate need for forgiveness from God. That's the starting point.

12.2 Apart from the forgiveness you receive from God, you are incapable of the kind of forgiveness required in this text.
